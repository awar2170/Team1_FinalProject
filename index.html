To eat or not to eat...

The purpose of our project is to use machine learning to determine whether a mushroom is poisonous or edible based on features provided by the user.

Our dataset was sourced from the Audubon Society who have extensive records of mushroom features that correlate to the toxicity of different species. We chose this topic because of the accuracy of the dataset and to provide a tool to promote health and safety.


Data Story

This Tableau storyboard shows the distribution of poisonous and edible mushrooms based on 6 different key features. Each predictor is color coded blue for "Edible" and orange for "Poisonous."

Our analysis of the data shows these features to have the largest impact on whether or not a mushroom is edible (image: Correlation of .5 or higher).

Based on our analysis of the data, we found that mushrooms with bruises, a tapering stalk shape, and evanescent ring types are more likely to be poisonous. While mushrooms with an enlarging stalk shape and large ring types are more likely to be edible.

Our odor analysis showed that mushrooms that smell creosote, fishy, musty, and pungent are more likely to be poisonous while mushrooms that smell like almond and anise are more likely to be edible. Mushrooms that have no smell are more likely to be edible, but have the possibility of being poisonous.

Lastly, when looking at the ring type of mushrooms we found that mushrooms with evanescent and pendant ring types were more likely to be edible while mushrooms with a large and no ring type tend to be poisonous.


Dataset

This pie chart shows the distribution of poisonous vs edible mushrooms in our dataset. The final data included 5,644 different mushroom varieties. This data uses 22 different features to define each mushroom.

Those features are:

  • cap shape, cap surface, cap color;
  • bruises, odor;
  • gill attachment, gill spacing, gill size, gill color;
  • stalk shape, stalk root;
  • stalk surface above ring, stalk surface below ring;
  • stalk color above ring, stalk color below ring;
  • veil type, veil color;
  • ring number, ring type;
  • spore print color, population, habitat


Data Determination

Our selected machine learning model XGBoost returned an accuracy score of 1.0 during training. This suggests that our data had clear correlations and was well-separated when determining if a mushroom is edible or not.
